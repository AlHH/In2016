About the project

 

This website provides an ‘encyclopaedia’ of how it felt to live in Egypt and Tunisia in 2016. Through a snapshot portrait of one year, it allows the user to ‘jump right into’ and move around (via cross-references) in post-“Arab Spring” realities, thus forming a picture of Arab life-worlds in this period of transition and historic change.

The work is the result of a research project based at the University of Oslo and carried out 2015-2018 by an international team of 27 scholars. Its major purpose is to highlight aspects of post-“Arab Spring” realities that tend to be neglected or heavily underrepresented in the media coverage as well as in mainstream academic treatment of the contemporary Arab world, dominated as they are by a focus on war and violence, Islamist extremism, autocratic rule, and humanitarian tragedy. While not ignoring these aspects, our work aims to redress this reductionist imbalance and to provide a richer, more varied picture.
To do so, it focuses on Arab everyday life-worlds as reflected in cultural production (fiction, cinema, music, architecture, visual art, …) and social media:

    • Cultural production is often particularly able to express significant sociocultural dynamics, including bodily experiences and emotions, and gives ready access to musings on topical issues and ‘the meaning of life’.
    • Social media allows us to gather what is ‘buzzing’, as well as provides a window on people’s primary, often emotional, reactions to what captures their attention.

These sources have been analysed with an eye to highlighting characteristic ‘arrays’ (dispositifs) that shaped Egyptians’ and/or Tunisians’ way of experiencing and categorizing their realities, and distinctive alternative choices that informed their way of looking at things and making decisions in life.

“In 2016" takes its methodological inspiration from Hans Ulrich Gumbrecht’s seminal study In 1926: Living on the Edge of Time (Cambridge, MA, 1997), applying his “one-year snapshot” approach to two key countries of the “Arab Spring”, Egypt and Tunisia. The focus is on specific topical and structural issues that appear prominently in our sources’ discussions of everyday experiences in 2016. Like Gumbrecht, our book presents a number of key entries:

  • "arrays" (ways in which artefacts, roles, and activities influence bodies),
  • “codes” (principles of order that inform discourses about the unstructured simultaneity of everyday-worlds),
  • and “collapsed codes” (which are showing signs of malfunction and therefore attract particular emotional attention).

Interlinked like a rhizome, these alphabetically arranged essays provide entry points and prisms onto the discursive and emotional realities of the contemporary Arab world.
